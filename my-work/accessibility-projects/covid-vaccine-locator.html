COVID Vaccine Locator Usability Testing

Population: Blind and Low-Vision Screen Reader Users

Research Team: Keslie Carrión, Jessica Borland, Prachi Newadkar, Claire Yeske

Institution: DePaul University

Date: November 2022

Full Research Paper: View Paper (PDF)

Project Overview

As the COVID-19 vaccine became available, national and local governments directed people to websites to find information about vaccine locations and appointments. People who are blind access websites with screen readers, which often skip over data visualizations like maps, charts, and graphs. Due to this, people who are blind have difficulty accessing critical information about COVID-19.

We conducted remote and in-person usability tests on the Chicago COVID-19 vaccine locator website with eight participants, four people who were blind and four who were sighted. We uncovered and analyzed usability and accessibility problems, including indirect pathways to information, lack of clear and concise language, and lack of user control and freedom. Our recommendations for future website design iterations include prioritizing information, reducing reliance on recall, consistency, and appropriate use of elements, alt tags, and ARIA.

"Figuring out how to schedule my COVID vaccine was confusing, now that I think about it, I didn’t even set mine up."

— Study Participant #6

Research Context & Motivation

The Problem

  • Vaccine websites were rapidly deployed without accessibility testing
  • Disabled individuals are at a higher risk from COVID-19 complications
  • Phone-based booking systems overwhelmed with demand
  • Online systems were the primary access method

Testing Goals

  • Identify accessibility barriers in Chicago's governement vaccine booking website
  • Understand screen reader user experiences and challenges
  • Provide actionable recommendations for improvement
  • Advocate for accessible healthcare technology

Testing Methodology

We recruited eight participants, four people who were blind and four people who were sighted, through personal and professional connections. The average age of participants who were blind was 68.75 years. Three participants who were blind resided in the Chicago area, while one resided outside the Chicago area. All the participants who were blind used JAWS as their primary screen reader. The average age of participants who were sighted was 29.3 years. Two participants who were sighted resided in the Chicago area, while two resided outside the Chicago area. All participants had an interest in improving access to COVID-19 vaccines.

Testing Procedure

We conducted usability tests remotely and in person. Each session lasted 30 to 45 minutes and had a moderator and notetaker. We began each session by reading the consent form to all participants, and we allowed for participants to ask questions and verbally consent to the study. Then, we asked participants about their experience finding information about COVID-19 vaccine locations, setting up appointments, and commuting to them.

We instructed participants to share their screens and navigate to the Chicago COVID-19 vaccine locator website. We asked participants to browse the website's homepage and describe their first impressions. We instructed participants who were blind to use their screen reader at their typical speed. Next, we asked participants to complete the following four tasks:

Usability Test Tasks

  1. Task 1: Imagine you want to find a place to get the COVID-19 vaccine. Using this website, please show me how to find a COVID-19 vaccine location near you?
  2. Task 2: You've heard that pharmacies such as CVS and Walgreens also offer vaccines. Please walk me through how you would find which pharmacies offer COVID-19 vaccines?
  3. Task 3: You received your first and second dose of the COVID vaccine about three months ago. You are looking to see if you can schedule your first booster (third shot). How would you go about figuring this out?
  4. Task 4: You've heard that Chicago is offering in-home vaccinations. Where would you find information about this, and how would you schedule an appointment?

Due to time constraints, we did not present Task 3 to one participant (P2), nor Task 4 to three participants (P2, P3, P5). After each task, we asked all participants to rate the task on a Likert scale of one to five, with one being easy and five being difficult. Additionally, we recorded whether participants experienced failure or success on each task.

To conclude, we asked all participants to rate the likelihood that they would use a website like the Chicago COVID-19 vaccine locator on a Likert scale of one to five, with one being unlikely and five being extremely likely. Finally, we asked open-ended questions to solicit additional feedback about their experience with the website.

Qualitative Findings

Based on observations and participant quotes from usability testing, we identified three key themes:

  • Indirect pathways to information
  • Unclear or inconsistent language
  • Limited user control and freedom

1. Indirect Pathways to Information

Most users began with the same navigation path: Find a Vaccine → Vaccine Finder.

All four sighted users (4/4) successfully completed Task 1 by using the map feature. In contrast, none of the blind users (0/4) were able to successfully complete this task due to an inability to navigate the map feature with their screen readers.

Key Issues:

  • Ctrl+F search functionality was unavailable on the website
  • 4 out of 8 users expressed desire to search directly by ZIP code
  • Search bar appeared too far down in the navigation order
  • Users relied on recall instead of recognition in later tasks

2. Lack of Clear and Concise Language

Terminology Confusion:

  • "Bivalent" was unclear to multiple participants
  • "Provider" was used inconsistently across different contexts

"I keep hearing about this 'bivalent' vaccine, but I have no idea what that means. Why can't they just say 'booster' or explain it?"

— Participant 3

Lack of ARIA Attributes:

  • Alt text announced irrelevant decorative images
  • Example: Every decorative star in the Chicago flag divider was read aloud by screen readers "seperator, Chicago star graphic, Chicago star graphic, Chicago star graphic, Chicago star graphic, seperator"

3. Lack of User Control and Freedom

Navigation Structure:

  • 6 of 9 main menu items were dropdown menus
  • Blind users became trapped in navigation loops and couldn't tab out

"There isn't any information here I can get to. I'm stuck and I don't know how to move forward."

— Participant 1

Interaction Barriers:

  • Pop-up windows lacked clear exit paths
  • Important content was unreachable by screen reader
  • Rotating homepage carousel was difficult to identify and use

Discussion

This study evaluated the usability and accessibility of Chicago's COVID-19 vaccine locator website for people who are blind or visually impaired and require the use of screen readers for computing.

Key Finding:

Blind participants experienced significantly more difficulty completing tasks than sighted participants, with a success rate disparity of 0/4 versus 4/4 on critical navigation tasks.

"I feel like I'm going in circles. I can't find what I'm looking for, and I can't get out of this menu."

— Participant 4, describing navigation loop frustration
Issue 1

Indirect Pathways to Information

The Problem

Screen readers:

  • Skipped critical data visualizations
  • Could not access the vaccine locator map
  • Had to resort to telephone booking

Why This Matters

Phone booking creates inequitable barriers:

  • Long wait times (often 30+ minutes)
  • Limited operating hours

Impact:

This leads to delays in vaccination access for blind users, creating health disparities.

Issue 2

Unclear and Inaccessible Language

Terminology Confusion

  • Unfamiliar terms like "bivalent" were not explained
  • Homepage readability difficulty score: 8.2 (above WCAG recommendation of 6.0)

Screen Reader Experience

  • Excessive alt text for decorative elements disrupted navigation
  • Missing alt text and ARIA tags blocked essential content

Impact:

Blind participants extracted less information and took significantly longer to complete tasks compared to sighted users.

Issue 3

Lack of User Control and Freedom

Screen Reader Navigation Tools Failed

  • Heading Navigation ("H" key): Returned empty results due to improper heading structure
  • Keyboard Navigation (Tab key): Users became trapped in sections and couldn't escape

Impact:

Prevented targeted navigation and efficient exploration of content, forcing users to listen to entire pages sequentially.

Implications for Design

Based on our findings, we identified six design recommendations to improve accessibility and usability for both sighted and non-sighted users.

1. Important Information First

  • Users want direct pathways to key content.

Recommendation:

  • Restructure information architecture so critical information appears first.

2. Reduce Reliance on Recall

  • Users relied on memory instead of visible cues.

Recommendation:

  • Keep essential links and content visible or easily retrievable.

3. Provide Accelerators

  • Users wanted search and shortcuts.

Recommendation:

  • Add early and persistent accelerators (e.g., enable Ctrl + F, visible search bars).

4. Define and Use Terminology Consistently

  • Terms like "provider" and "bivalent" caused confusion.

Recommendation:

  • Use clear language and define essential terms consistently.

5. Use ALT and ARIA with Discretion

  • Screen readers announced decorative images excessively.

Recommendation:

  • Remove or simplify irrelevant alt text and ARIA labels to reduce listening fatigue.

6. Choose Appropriate UI Elements

  • Dropdown menus and carousels blocked access.

Recommendation:

  • Use screen-reader-friendly components to improve control and navigation.
